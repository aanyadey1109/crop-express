OCA

ABOUT | HISTORY | NATIVE ROOTS

Oca (Oxalis tuberosa) is a traditional tuber crop native to the Andean highlands of South America, particularly cultivated in countries like Peru, Bolivia, and Ecuador. Revered for centuries by Indigenous communities, oca was a staple crop alongside potatoes and quinoa, thriving in the challenging terrains of the Andes. Its cultural significance extends beyond its nutritional value, as it is often associated with traditional farming practices and local cuisines. The vibrant colors and tangy flavor of its tubers made it an essential part of Andean agriculture and culinary heritage.

WHY IS OCA ENDANGERED?

Oca faces endangerment due to several factors, including the loss of traditional farming knowledge, changing dietary habits, and the rise of commercial monoculture crops. Modern agricultural systems prioritize high-yield crops like potatoes, displacing traditional Andean tubers. Climate change also poses a significant threat, as it disrupts the delicate high-altitude ecosystems where oca thrives. Additionally, the global lack of awareness and demand for this crop limits its cultivation outside its native regions, further jeopardizing its survival.

SOIL REQUIREMENTS | NUTRITIONAL VALUES

Oca is well-suited for loamy, well-drained soils with high organic content and moderate acidity, replicating the conditions of its native Andean environments. It requires cool temperatures and can endure poor soils better than many other crops. Nutritionally, oca is rich in carbohydrates, especially complex sugars, and offers a good source of vitamin C, potassium, and dietary fiber. Its low-calorie profile and antioxidant properties make it a valuable addition to healthy diets, emphasizing its potential as a nutrient-dense, climate-resilient crop.
