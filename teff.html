TEFF

ABOUT | HISTORY | NATIVE ROOTS

Teff (Eragrostis tef), an ancient grain native to Ethiopia and Eritrea, has been a staple food for over 3,000 years. Known as a resilient and versatile crop, it thrives in diverse environments, making it central to Ethiopian culture and cuisine. Traditionally used to prepare injera, a sourdough flatbread, teff holds significant cultural and historical value. Its small seeds, believed to symbolize resilience, have also earned it the nickname "lost crop of Africa" for its relatively low international recognition until recent years.

WHY IS AMARANTH ENDANGERED?

Despite its historical importance, teff faces endangerment due to factors like climate change, unsustainable farming practices, and monoculture reliance in its native regions. Soil degradation, drought, and increasing competition for agricultural land further threaten its cultivation. Additionally, the global commercialization of teff has led to issues like overharvesting and patent disputes, limiting access for local farmers and potentially jeopardizing traditional farming practices.

SOIL REQUIREMENTS | NUTRITIONAL VALUES

Teff thrives in well-drained, loamy soils with moderate fertility and a slightly acidic to neutral pH. It is drought-resistant and grows in various climatic conditions, making it an adaptable crop. Nutritionally, teff is a powerhouse. It is rich in protein, dietary fiber, and essential amino acids, including lysine, often lacking in other grains. It also contains significant amounts of iron, calcium, and B vitamins, making it a nutritious option for gluten-free and health-conscious diets.
